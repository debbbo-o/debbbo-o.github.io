History of Pears

Introduction to pears

Pears, are a typical from from temperate climates, have a delicate, smooth and pleasant taste. It is very commonly found in many countries as the fifth most widely produced fruit in the world, being produced mainly in China, Europe, and the United States. It appears in many different cuisines and dishes, from salads to desserts (eg. pies, jams, ice cream) and is even great when being paired with various meats (eg. lamb or bacon).


Origin of pears

The name 'pear' is derived from the Latin word, pira, which means "fruit".
Pears have two different origin and domestication points, China and Asia Minor until the Middle East. Pears are one of the oldest cultivated and beloved fruits in the world, dating back to 5000 BC and have been grown in China from as early as 2000 BC.
Historians say that 7,000 years ago, in the foothills of Tien Shan, a Chinese diplomat became obsessed with pears. His fascination with the sweet fruit led him to abandon his career and begin a business of grafting and selling pear trees.


Top pear producing countries

1. China
China is the world's largest pear producer with around 17 million tonnes of pears per year which means that China alone produces more than 70% of the world's pears.

2. Argentina
Argentina comes second in pear production with about 930 thousand tonnes of pears per annum.

3. Italy
Italy is third in the world in pear production, producing about 770 thousand tonnes of pears per year.

Types of Pears

There are more than approximately 3000 pear varieties grown worldwide. There are different types of pears grown in different regions all have varying characteristics like appearance, softness and sweetness.
Asian pears are native to Japan and China where they have been grown for over 3000 years. Chinese pears are called "LÃ­" and are round with smooth, yellow skin while the Japanese pears are called "Nashi" which has a longer and more oblong shape with thin brown skin.

Examples of Asian pears

Hosui Pear:
The Hosui pear has a snappy and tangy taste with a slightly higher acid content than other Asian pears. It is really sweet and crisp, similar to an apple, and has really high water content.


Shinseiki Pear:
This pear has a cream-coloured, crisp and juicy flesh with a sweet flavour which makes it ideal for fruit salads and snacking. The fruit itself is usually round but sometimes lopsided and has a longer stem with bright yellow skin.


Examples of Pears from USA

Bartlett Pear:
The Bartlett is extremely soft and juicy with an extremely sweet flesh when ripe. It has a yellowish-brown exterior and is great when paired with different meats like steak and meatballs.

Bosc Pear:
The pear is often more crisp than soft and is rather tart compared to other pears. It has a brown skin and is more of an oval shape with its long body.

Nutritional Value of Pears

Pears are an exceptional source of natural nutrients from different essential vitamins to dietary fibre. It is only 100 calories for a medium sized pear which packs 21% daily value for dietary fibre, 8% Vitamin C and 4% potassium.

Dietary Fibre
Pears are one of the best fruits to consume because of its high value of dietary fibre. Fibre helps to maintain balance, regularity and reduces the risk of colorectal cancer. It slows digestion in the upper gastrointestinal track which helps to prolong the feeling of fullness and also slows absorption of sugars which reduces sugar spikes and cravings.

Potassium
Potassium is important as it is necessary for muscle contractions, including a regular heartbeat, and nerve impulses which helps with reflexes. It also helps regulate fluid balance by osmosis within cells. Poor fuild balance can lead to dehydration which in turn, can affect the heart and kidneys.

Vitamin C
Vitamin C, which is also known as ascorbic acid, is essential for the growth, development and repair of all the body tissues. It is involved in many body functions, such as the formation of collagen, absorption of iron, proper functioning of the immune system, wound healing, and maintaenance of cartilage, bones and even teeth.

How are Pears Grown


Germination

Each mature fruit contains up to ten sleek, black seeds covered with a thin endosperm coating. The tough seed coat holds the embryo, endosperm, cotyledons, as well as the young root and shoot. Pear seeds usually begin to sprout and produce green leaves within 3 months, but some sprouts may emerge as early as 18 days after planting. Germination starts when the seed coat absorbs water, causing it to expand and crack open. The first sign of life is the radicle, a small white tail that grows into the plant's primary root. The radicle's purpose is to anchor the plant securely in the soil and initiate water absorption. As the seed absorbs water, a stem emerges, and the cotyledons start to unfurl. These cotyledons are the initial leaves of the seedlings. True leaves, similar to those of a fully grown pear tree, appear several days later. Once the seedlings have at least four true leaves, they are ready for transplantation.

Juvenile

During the early phase of growth, a pear seedling remains in its vegetative stage, and it typically takes at least four years before the tree starts producing blooms. Sometimes, this blooming process can take as long as 14 years. Young pear trees grow vertically, sporting dense foliage that may resemble thorny plants. Initially, the bark is smooth, but as the tree matures, it becomes tougher. Meanwhile, the roots also experience rapid growth. Pear leaves are glossy and dark green, featuring lighter green veins that extend from the leaf center to its edges. The undersides of the leaves have a non-shiny texture and a lighter hue. As the pear tree reaches maturity, its leaves take on a teardrop shape with sharp edges and a rounded base. The leaf edges are slightly serrated. The bark of the mature pear tree appears grey-brown, but over time, it transforms into a scaly texture.

Flowering

Pear flowers appear as small, closed green buds with an oval to rectangular shape on the tree's dark brown branches. When the spring temperatures rise, these buds open up, revealing broad, flat, and velvety white petals with a slightly wavy look. Usually, pear blossoms grow in groups of five to seven and emit a pleasant and fresh fragrance. Bees play a crucial role in pollination by transferring pollen between different pear varieties, aiding in the optimal development of fruits. Although pear flowers bloom for a brief period and offer limited nectar to bees, bees remain the main pollinators. Additional pollinators may be necessary for pear trees to yield fruits successfully. Insufficient pollination can lead to early fruit shedding, and the fruits that do grow might be small and misshapen. Conversely, with proper pollination, the outcome is the production of large and well-formed pears.

Fruiting

After successful pollination, a cluster of five to eight fruits forms, surrounded by leaves. It takes approximately 115 to 165 days, or three to five months, from full bloom to harvest. The majority of the fruit is made up of the base of the floral tube, the pseudocarp, and the floret receptacles. Fruit size is influenced by crucial processes like cell division and expansion. During the cell expansion stage, the cells absorb abundant water, leading to a rapid increase in fruit size. When fully mature, the pear fruit has an elongated shape, narrow at the stem end, and wider at the opposite end. Pears come in various colors, including green, red, yellow, gold, and brown varieties.

Maturity

As the days shorten and temperatures decrease, the pear tree naturally pauses its growth as part of its annual cycle. During this time, the tree sheds its leaves and enters a period of dormancy or rest. When the weather warms up again, the bud initiation process starts anew, marking the beginning of a new cycle of blooming and fruiting. Typically, pear trees bloom from late February to mid-April, and the fruit is produced from mid-August to mid-October. With favorable conditions, pear trees have the potential to live for around 50 years.
